Background

NYC COVID Trends

It’s difficult to overstate the extent to which the COVID-19 pandemic has tested the world’s public health infrastructure over the past two years. At the same time, the COVID-19 “experience” has manifested unequally -- not just country to country, but city to city, and even borough to borough.

Explore

NYC COVID Trends

As one of the most heterogeneous urban areas in the world, New York City provides a fascinating case study into the ways socioeconomic status may be associated with, or even mediate, disparities in health outcomes.

Project Focus

NYC COVID Trends

Knowing that socioeconomic factors have historically been associated with health outcomes, our aim was to examine relationships between a range of predictors (e.g. race/ethnicity, education, broadband internet access, household income / occupational income score, public vs. private health insurance) and COVID-19 health outcomes -- namely, hospitalizations, deaths, and vaccinations.

Main Questions

NYC COVID Trends

Our initial questions were centered on how demographic factors in New York City trend with COVID-19 outcomes. Particularly, what are the key demographic correlates (e.g. race/ethnicity, education) of COVID-19 hospitalizations or vaccinations? Broadly, we aimed to explore the myriad relationships between predictors and COVID-19 outcomes across New York City census tracts and boroughs.

Take Aways

NYC COVID Trends

In an analysis of correlations between predictors and outcomes across PUMA geographies, we found income to be strongly associated with COVID-19 hospitalizations (negative association), deaths (negative association), and vaccinations (positive association). We found that many indicators of socioeconomic status – including poverty level, welfare, unemployment, and food stamp utilization – seem highly negatively correlated with vaccination, but not with hospitalization and death.

Data Sources

NYC COVID Trends

We obtained census data from the Integrated Public Use Microdata Series (IPUMS USA), COVID-19 outcome data from the New York City Department of Health and Mental Hygiene (NYC DOHMH), and used a crosswalk data set from Baruch College, City University of New York - Geoportal to combine these data.
